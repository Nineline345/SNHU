### Briefly summarize the DriverPass project. Who was the client? What type of system did they want you to design?
The client was DriverPass, a company seeking to incorporate a web-based system to help students pass their DMV Driver's License test. They aimed to do this by providing online practice tests, in-person driving instruction, and progress reports to identify areas where the student has room for improvement.

### How did you interpret the user’s needs and implement them into your system design? Why is it so important to consider the user’s needs when designing?
I approached the user's needs by considering what would happen from the moment they clicked the DriverPass website until they left it. This enabled me to treat each function of the DriverPass system as a near-linear flow that starts and finishes with the same thing. Additionally, I also considered my personal experiences as a user and what was most frustrating to me. Considering the user's needs is crucial to reducing revisions in the system and ensuring our efforts align with real-world tasks and requirements.

### How do you approach designing software? What techniques or strategies would you use in the future to analyze and design a system?
Prior to this course, my only experience in designing software was in game development. I started the design by building a Game Design Document (GDD). Once that was complete, I started working on pieces considered foundational to game development, such as procedural terrain generation, the sim-speed/time system, and the entity component system. Going forward, I will use significantly more UML diagrams to support my development processes and tracking. It would be especially valuable in pieces that are vital and complex. For example, the terrain generation previously mentioned required a Tile Schema with Chunking, Global world coordinates, automated tile layering and masking, biome/region tags, and a renderer that culls off-screen tiles while keeping their metadata fully simulated and mutable to maintain the game's "living and breathing world" concept.
